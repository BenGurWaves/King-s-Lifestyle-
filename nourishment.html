LESSON 02

Clean Design Decoded — The Split Hoof and Fin Tests

God did not hand Moses a spreadsheet of 10,000 species and say “memorize these.” Instead, He gave two elegant filters — biological markers that any shepherd, fisherman, or king could apply instantly. For land animals: does it have a split hoof and chew the cud? For sea creatures: does it have fins and scales? Two questions. Thousands of species sorted in seconds.

The brilliance is in the simplicity. A cow splits the hoof and chews the cud — clean. A pig splits the hoof but does not chew the cud — unclean. A horse chews but does not split — unclean. Both markers must be present. This is not arbitrary. Animals that pass both tests are herbivorous ruminants with multi-chamber stomachs that thoroughly process plant material, filtering toxins through four stages of digestion before the nutrients reach the meat you consume.

For seafood, the fin-and-scale test eliminates bottom-feeders, filter-feeders, and scavengers — creatures whose biological purpose is to clean the ocean floor. Shrimp, crab, lobster, catfish, and oysters are the garbage disposal systems of the sea. Eating them is eating the filter. Salmon, cod, trout, and tilapia pass the test — they are the ocean’s clean protein.

Once you internalize these two filters, every menu, every grocery aisle, every restaurant becomes navigable in seconds. You are not checking an app. You are applying the Creator’s design logic.

“You may eat any animal that has a divided hoof and that chews the cud. Of all the creatures living in the water, you may eat any that has fins and scales.”

— Leviticus 11:3,9

LESSON 04

The Forbidden Swine — Why Pork Has No Place on the King’s Table

Of all the dietary laws, the prohibition against pork generates the most resistance. Bacon is cultural. Ham is holiday tradition. Pork chops are comfort food. But comfort and truth are not the same thing. The pig splits the hoof but does not chew the cud — it fails the test in Leviticus 11:7 specifically, as if God anticipated the argument and named the animal directly.

The pig’s digestive system is a single-chamber stomach that processes food in roughly four hours. A cow’s ruminant system takes up to 80 hours across four chambers. The difference matters. A pig absorbs toxins, parasites, and whatever it consumed — and pigs are omnivorous scavengers that will eat anything, including their own waste — directly into the meat. There is no multi-stage filtration. What goes in comes through.

Trichinella spiralis, the parasitic roundworm associated with pork, can survive in undercooked pork and cause trichinosis — muscle pain, fever, and in severe cases, cardiac complications. Modern cooking temperatures reduce this risk, but the biological reality remains: pork carries parasitic and inflammatory loads that clean animals do not.

The swap is simple and immediate. Bacon becomes turkey bacon or beef bacon. Ham becomes roasted lamb or smoked turkey breast. Pork sausage becomes chicken or beef sausage. You lose nothing in flavor and gain everything in alignment. The hardest part is the first two weeks. After that, you will not miss it.

“And the pig, though it has a divided hoof, does not chew the cud; it is unclean for you.”

— Leviticus 11:7

LESSON 05

Ocean Kings — Mastering Clean Seafood

The ocean is divided into two kingdoms: the clean swimmers and the floor cleaners. Fins and scales mark the difference. Wild-caught salmon, with its deep orange flesh and dense omega-3 profile, sits at the top of the clean seafood hierarchy. It is one of the most nutrient-dense foods on earth — high in EPA and DHA, rich in astaxanthin, loaded with B vitamins and selenium.

But not all salmon is created equal. Farm-raised salmon is fed pellets of corn, soy, and ground-up fish parts. It is dyed pink (its flesh is naturally grey). It contains higher levels of PCBs, dioxins, and inflammatory omega-6 fatty acids. Wild-caught Alaskan sockeye is the gold standard. It costs more per pound, but a king does not economize on the fuel for his temple.

Beyond salmon, your clean seafood options are vast: cod, halibut, trout, tilapia, mahi-mahi, snapper, bass, sardines, herring, and anchovies. Sardines and anchovies are among the most nutrient-dense foods available — small fish with short lifespans that accumulate minimal mercury. Two cans of sardines per week provide more omega-3s than most supplements.

At King Soopers or Whole Foods in Aurora, the seafood counter is your proving ground. Ask one question: “Is this wild-caught?” If the answer is vague, move on. If the label says “farm-raised,” check the source country. Norwegian and Scottish farms have better standards than most, but wild-caught remains superior. A king sources the best available, not the most convenient.

“Of all the creatures living in the water of the seas and the streams, you may eat any that have fins and scales.”

— Leviticus 11:9

LESSON 06

Birds of the Throne — Clean Poultry and the Cricket Question

When God fed Israel in the wilderness, He sent quail. Not random birds — quail. Clean, ground-nesting, seed-eating birds that fit the pattern of every clean animal: they consume plants, not carrion. The birds prohibited in Leviticus 11 are predators and scavengers — eagles, vultures, hawks, owls, storks. They eat other animals, often dead ones. The principle is consistent: do not eat the eater of death.

Your clean poultry list is practical and affordable: chicken, turkey, quail, duck, goose, pheasant, and Cornish game hen. These birds are herbivorous or omnivorous with plant-heavy diets. They are widely available, budget-friendly, and versatile in the kitchen. A rotisserie chicken from Costco for $4.99 feeds a family and passes the test.

Now, the modern question: insects. Cricket flour is being marketed as the next superfood. Leviticus 11:22 permits certain locusts, crickets, and grasshoppers — those with jointed legs for hopping. This is one of the few areas where the law is more permissive than most people expect. However, commercial cricket flour in 2026 often contains additives and is processed in facilities that handle unclean ingredients. If you pursue this, source matters.

The broader principle: a king does not eat what eats death. Vultures consume rotting flesh. Hawks tear living prey. The birds on your plate should be those that ate seeds, grains, and plants — clean fuel processed through a clean system into clean meat.

LESSON 08

The Gentleman’s Table — Eating with Restraint and Dignity

Solomon, the wealthiest king in history, wrote a warning about the dinner table: “When you sit to dine with a ruler, note well what is before you, and put a knife to your throat if you are given to gluttony.” The knife-to-throat imagery is deliberate. Solomon understood that a man who cannot control his appetite will eventually lose control of his kingdom. The table is a test of character.

Gluttony is not simply eating too much. It is eating without awareness, without gratitude, without restraint. It is scrolling your phone while shoveling food. It is eating standing over the kitchen counter at 11 PM. It is consuming a meal in four minutes that took forty to prepare. A king eats with presence. He sits. He pauses. He tastes. He stops when satisfied, not when stuffed.

Table manners are not elitism — they are discipline made visible. How you eat reveals how you live. The man who bolts his food bolts his decisions. The man who eats mindlessly lives mindlessly. The man who shows gratitude at the table shows gratitude in every room he enters. Your conduct at meals is a microcosm of your conduct in life.

In 2026, the average American eats 20% of meals in the car. A king never eats in the car unless necessity demands it. A king sits at a table, blesses the food, engages in conversation if company is present, and treats the meal as what it is: a sacred act of fueling the temple. This is not religious performance. It is practical dignity.

“When you sit to dine with a ruler, note well what is before you, and put a knife to your throat if you are given to gluttony.”

— Proverbs 23:1–2

LESSON 09

The table will teach you what no parenting book can. Provision is not just food on the plate — it is presence at the table.

Visit a local butcher in Aurora or Denver. Ask questions. Learn the names. Handle the cuts. A butcher who sees a customer asking intelligent questions will give you better service, better cuts, and better advice than the self-serve case at the supermarket. Know your food from field to plate. That is the king’s way.

LESSON 20

Recovery Fuel — Feeding the Body After Battle

“She gets up while it is still night; she provides food for her family and portions for her female servants.” The Proverbs 31 woman prepared provisions before the day began. The principle applies to your post-workout nutrition: the preparation must precede the need. When you finish training, your body enters a recovery window — 30 to 60 minutes where protein synthesis is elevated and glycogen stores are depleted. What you eat in this window determines whether you built muscle or wasted effort.

The post-workout meal is the most important meal of the day for anyone who trains seriously. Within 30 minutes of finishing: 30-40 grams of clean protein (grilled chicken, eggs, a clean protein shake) and 40-60 grams of complex carbohydrates (sweet potato, rice, oats). This combination replenishes glycogen and provides amino acids for muscle repair. Skip this window, and recovery slows by hours.

Pre-workout nutrition matters equally. Two to three hours before training: a balanced meal of clean protein, complex carbs, and vegetables. One hour before: a smaller snack if needed — a banana with almond butter, a handful of nuts. Training on a completely empty stomach can work for low-intensity sessions, but for heavy lifting or intense conditioning, fuel the machine before you stress it.

Hydration is non-negotiable. Half your body weight in ounces of water daily, minimum. Add electrolytes during and after intense training — a pinch of sea salt in water, or a clean electrolyte powder without artificial sweeteners. The temple that trains must be fueled and hydrated with the same precision applied to every other aspect of the blueprint.

Energy That Lasts — Blood, Fat, and the Prohibition Principle

“This is a lasting ordinance for the generations to come, wherever you live: You must not eat any fat or any blood.” Two prohibitions that bridge the Old and New Testaments. The Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 retained the blood prohibition for Gentile believers. These are not cultural relics — they are lasting ordinances with biological and spiritual significance.

The blood prohibition is universal in scope. Blood carries pathogens, toxins, and waste products that the animal’s body was in the process of filtering. Consuming blood increases exposure to bloodborne parasites and bacteria. Proper draining of blood from meat (kosher or halal slaughter practices) reduces this risk. When buying meat, well-drained cuts from reputable butchers align with this principle.

The fat prohibition specifically addresses chelev — the hard, suet-like fat surrounding the kidneys and organs. This is distinct from marbling fat within the muscle (which is permitted). Organ fat accumulates toxins that the animal filtered throughout its life. The distinction matters: a well-marbled ribeye is clean; pure suet rendered from organ fat violates the principle.

Energy that lasts comes from clean fuel properly prepared. Complex carbohydrates for sustained glucose release. Clean proteins for amino acid delivery. Healthy fats — olive oil, avocado, nuts, the natural marbling in clean meat — for hormone production and satiety. When you eliminate the prohibited and optimize the permitted, your energy stabilizes, your inflammation drops, and your performance rises. The blueprint produces results because the Engineer knew the machine.
